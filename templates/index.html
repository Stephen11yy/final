General Information

Demographic

>> Age:
The risk of heart disease increases with age. Men over the age of 45 and women over the age of 55 are at higher risk of developing heart disease.
>> Gender:
Men are more likely to develop heart disease than premenopausal women. However, after menopause, women's risk of heart disease increases, and it becomes similar to that of men.

Medical

>> trestbps (Hypertension):
High blood pressure can increase the workload on the heart and blood vessels, leading to the development of heart disease.
>> High Cholesterol:
High levels of cholesterol in the blood can contribute to the development of plaque in the arteries, increasing the risk of heart disease.
>> fbs (Diabetes):
People with diabetes are at higher risk of developing heart disease due to the impact of high blood sugar levels on blood vessels and the heart.
>> Family History of Heart Disease:
Having a close family member, such as a parent or sibling, with heart disease increases an individual's risk of developing heart disease.
>> Chest pain (cp):
Chest pain is often the first warning sign of a heart condition. Patients with typical angina or asymptomatic signs are more likely to be diagnosed with heart disease
>> restecg ():
Abnormal ECG results are one of the earliest non-invasive indicators of heart issues. Conditions like ST-T abnormalities can signal ischemia (restricted blood flow), while LVH (left ventricular hypertrophy) can suggest long-term high blood pressure or valve disease.
>> thalach (Maxheartrate):
thalach is often one of the most important predictors of heart disease. It has a strong inverse relationship: lower thalach â†’ higher chance of heart issues.
>> exang (Exercise-induced angina):
Exercise-induced angina usually indicates a blockage in the coronary arteries, limiting blood supply to the heart during physical exertion.
>> oldpeak (depression):
The higher the value of oldpeak, the more significant the depression, The greater the depression, the higher the likelihood of heart disease.
>> slope():
Down-sloping ST segments are often a sign of severe heart disease and coronary artery blockages. Up-sloping and flat slopes are generally less concerning, though flat slopes can still signal early signs of heart disease.
